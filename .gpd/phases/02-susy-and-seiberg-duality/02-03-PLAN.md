    Section 2: The Coulomb Branch (~10 min)
    - In N=2 theories, the scalar Phi in the vector multiplet can acquire a VEV: <Phi> = diag(a_1, ..., a_{N_c}) with sum a_i = 0.
    - This breaks SU(N_c) -> U(1)^{N_c - 1} at a generic point (Coulomb phase).
    - The moduli space parametrised by gauge-invariant coordinates u_k = <Tr Phi^k> for k = 2, ..., N_c is called the Coulomb branch.
    - For SU(2): one complex parameter u = <Tr Phi^2>. The Coulomb branch is a complex plane parametrised by u.
    - Classical Coulomb branch has singular points where additional particles become massless:
      * At u = 0: W-bosons become massless (non-abelian enhancement).
      * Quantum mechanically: the singular point SPLITS into multiple points.

    Section 3: Seiberg-Witten Solution -- Executive Summary (~10 min)
    - STATE (do NOT derive): Seiberg and Witten (1994) showed that for N=2 SU(2) pure gauge theory:
      * The exact prepotential F(a) is determined by an auxiliary elliptic curve (the SW curve).
      * The Coulomb branch has TWO singular points (at u = +/- Lambda^2) where:
        - u = +Lambda^2: a magnetic MONOPOLE becomes massless.
        - u = -Lambda^2: a DYON (charge (1,1)) becomes massless.
      * At these points, the low-energy theory is a U(1) gauge theory coupled to the massless monopole/dyon.
    - For SU(N_c) with N_f hypermultiplets (SW II, 1994):
      * The Coulomb branch has a richer structure with multiple singular loci.
      * At each singular locus, specific magnetic/dyonic states become massless.
    - For SU(N_c) with N_f = 2N_c - 2 hypermultiplets (the maximally conformal case): there exist singular points where N_f MONOPOLES become simultaneously massless.
    - Physical picture: include a FIGURE (tikz) showing the Coulomb branch of SU(2) with the two singular points marked and the massless states labelled.
    - The monopoles carry quantum numbers under the flavour symmetry because the hypermultiplet quarks carry flavour charge and the monopole BPS mass formula involves the quark VEVs.

    Section 4: The Fayet-Iliopoulos Mechanism (~5 min)
    - Define the FI D-term: for a U(1) gauge factor, one can add V_FI = xi D to the Lagrangian, where D is the auxiliary field and xi is a real parameter with [xi] = [mass]^2.
    - In N=1 SUSY: V_D = (1/2)(g sum_i q_i |phi_i|^2 + xi)^2. Nonzero xi forces <phi> != 0, breaking the gauge symmetry.
    - In N=2 context: the U(1) factors on the Coulomb branch can have FI terms generated by mass deformations or quantum effects.
    - This provides a mechanism for the monopoles on the Coulomb branch to condense (acquire VEVs) when xi != 0, leading to confinement.
    - Historical note: cite Fayet-Iliopoulos, Phys. Lett. B 51, 461 (1974).

    Section 5: The APS Deformation -- From N=2 to N=1 (~10 min)
    - The key idea (Argyres-Plesser-Seiberg, 1996):
      1. Start with N=2 SU(N_c) SQCD with N_f hypermultiplets.
      2. Add a deformation: W_def = mu Tr Phi^2. This breaks N=2 -> N=1 (the adjoint mass).
      3. As mu -> infinity, the adjoint Phi becomes infinitely heavy and decouples.
      4. What remains is N=1 SU(N_c) SQCD with N_f flavours -- exactly the electric theory of Seiberg duality!
    - But we know what happens in the N=2 theory: at specific Coulomb branch points, monopoles/dyons become massless. When we add the deformation and send mu -> infinity:
      * The Coulomb branch is LIFTED (the deformation generates a potential for u_k).
      * The theory is driven to the specific singular points where monopoles are massless.
      * These massless monopoles/dyons BECOME the magnetic quarks q, q-tilde of N=1 Seiberg duality!
    - This is the physical origin of the magnetic degrees of freedom: they are the 't Hooft-Polyakov monopoles of the UV theory, made light by the N=2 dynamics.
    - The meson field M of the magnetic theory arises from the adjoint scalar Phi: as mu -> infinity, the massive components of Phi decouple, but the meson-like composite Q Phi Q-tilde survives.
    - Statement (cite APS): "Seiberg duality for N=1 SQCD can be derived as a limiting case of exact N=2 results. The magnetic quarks are monopoles."

    Section 6: Summary and Physical Picture (~5 min)

      1. N=2 SQCD is exactly solvable (SW).
      2. On the Coulomb branch, monopoles become massless at special points.
      3. The deformation W = mu Tr Phi^2 breaks N=2 -> N=1 and drives the theory to these points.
      4. The massless monopoles become the magnetic quarks of N=1 Seiberg duality.
      5. This explains WHERE the magnetic degrees of freedom come from and WHY they carry flavour quantum numbers.
